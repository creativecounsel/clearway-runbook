# Language Patterns to Watch

Common phrasing patterns that create avoidable legal risk, with a quick fix for each. Pulled out on its own as a fast self-check before submitting a draft.

| Instead of... | What trips people up about it | Easy fix |
|---|---|---|
| Judging the user or labeling accounts/people as "good," "bad," or "risky" | The company is describing behavior or data, not character. Labeling people invites profiling and decisioning risk; labeling accounts implies the company is the decision-maker. | Describe the behavior or the data point. "Shows signs of unusual activity." "Likelihood this listing is flagged for review." |
| Using a generic word that happens to also be a specific product name | If a product is named "Pulse" or "Compass," using that word generically blurs the line between the product and the concept, and risks weakening it as a protectable mark. | Reserve the word for the actual product. Use a different generic term (e.g., "indicators," "component," "infrastructure") everywhere else. |
| Stating a number without a qualifier ("91% pass rate," "25% conversion") | An unqualified number can read as a universal claim when it's actually scoped to a specific population, time period, or condition — this is a common source of deceptive-advertising risk. | Scope the number. State what population/time period it reflects, and use "up to" where appropriate. |
| Making the company the actor ("the company approves," "decides," "qualifies") | If the company provides inputs, options, or recommendations and the customer or end user makes the actual decision, saying the company "approves" or "decides" overstates the company's role and can create liability for outcomes the company doesn't control. | Make the customer/end user the actor. "Helps," "suggests," "provides options to support [their] decision." |
| Branding internal practices as "responsible," "ethical," or similar virtue language | These are loaded claims that regulators, customers, and governance teams will read closely and may ask you to substantiate. | Use specific, descriptive language instead ("trusted," "thoughtful," "built for [use case]") and describe what's actually true (e.g., what data a model is trained on) rather than asserting a virtue. |
| Calling something "AI-powered" or "ML-powered" generically | Not everything that could plausibly be called AI/ML uses it the same way — some features use traditional rule-based logic with limited ML elements. Overstating this is sometimes called "AI-washing" and carries its own regulatory and reputational risk. | Confirm with the relevant technical owner whether the specific feature actually uses AI/ML as described, and stay specific rather than applying the label broadly. |
| Using absolute or guarantee language ("eliminates," "ensures," "always," "never," "fully automated") | Absolute claims are rarely true without exception and are a common source of risk once a single counterexample exists. | Use bounded language ("helps reduce," "is designed to," "in most cases") unless a specific claim has been reviewed and cleared as an exception. |

---

*This list reflects recurring patterns, not a complete list. If something doesn't fit a pattern above but still feels risky, that instinct is usually right — flag it.*
